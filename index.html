Detroit Traffic Injury Analysis

How roadway design relates to severe crashes on Detroit’s most dangerous streets.

Project Overview

This project examines how roadway design influences the severity of traffic crashes in Detroit, with a focus on identifying patterns within the city’s High Injury Network (HIN). Using crash data analysis, the study finds that structural roadway features—particularly the number of lanes and intersection complexity are more strongly associated with serious and fatal injuries than posted speed limits. Wider, multi-lane roads and signalized intersections were shown to produce disproportionately severe outcomes, while speed limits alone had little statistical impact. Additionally, the analysis reveals that a significant share of severe crashes is concentrated on a small number of major arterial corridors, such as Gratiot, Greenfield, and 7 Mile. These findings highlight how historical urban planning decisions continue to shape present-day safety risks. The report ultimately recommends targeted, data-driven interventions—such as road diets and improved signal timing—on the most dangerous corridors and intersections to reduce severe injuries and improve community safety.

The Problem statement

Detroit has often been cited as one of the worst cities for commuters. This is due to frequent vehicle and pedestrian incidents, particularly at major intersections and on busy roadways. For instance, in 2023 Detroit experienced 24,344 traffic accidents across the city, resulting in almost 8,700 injuries and 130 fatalities. Concerns over community health and safety have been raised due to the city’s high traffic volume and contributing road characteristics, including lane configuration, lighting, and speed limits. For instance, the Detroit High injury network, designated by the city of Detroit's Department Public Works, identifies streets or intersections as one of the highest concentrations of traffic crashes. One such area in Livernois Ave, located between 7 mile Road and McNichols, is reported as one of the most dangerous areas because it experiences 23 accidents per mile and leads Wayne county in pedestrian fatalities. Moreover Detroit's community safety is significantly impacted by traffic fatalities. For instance, Detroit has one of the highest pedestrian fatality rates among U.S. ​​cities, with 28.71 deaths per 100,000 people and 6.16 pedestrian deaths per 100,000 people in 2020, ranking second and third nationally, respectively​. This issue directly affects communities located near major intersections and highways, like I-75, Gratiot Ave, Grand River, and 8 Mile where local pedestrians and commuters face higher risks of crashes and injuries. This pattern was a result of past urban planning and infrastructure developments, as the city prioritized vehicle throughput over safety. A great example would be the construction of I-75 and I-375, where communities like black bottom and paradise valley were destroyed in the process. This legacy persists today, as the I-375 construction severed the local street grid, creating a barrier that both funneled high volumes of traffic onto adjacent surface streets and resulted in outdated, dangerous interchange designs. Complex interchanges also elevate crash risk by increasing drivers’ cognitive workload, adding multiple decision points, and creating frequent merging and abrupt lane changes that make errors more likely and increase the probability of collisions.​ This can impact community health as those crashes can lead to injuries and fatalities, directly affecting the physical health of community members. Moreover, some roads that pass through communities have certain characteristics that can increase a potential for a motor vehicle and pedestrian accidents, such as multiple lanes, complex intersections, and heavy traffic.

Key Findings

  • Wider roads were linked to more severe crashes.
  • Speed limit did not show a strong relationship with severity.
  • A small number of streets and intersections accounted for many severe crashes.

Recommendations

  • Prioritize the highest-risk corridors for design improvements.
  • Review signalized intersections with repeated severe crashes.
  • Use the High Injury Network to guide safety investments.
