High-Risk Areas in Detroit

Where severe crashes are concentrated and why these corridors matter.

Why Location Matters

This page shows where Detroit's most dangerous crashes are concentrated. The data makes it clear that severe injuries are not spread evenly across the city. Instead, they cluster along a small group of major corridors and intersections that carry heavy traffic, have multiple lanes, and often include complex roadway designs.

These high-risk areas are important because they represent the physical spaces where design choices have the greatest impact on safety. By identifying the streets and intersections with the highest concentration of fatal and serious injuries, this project helps show where targeted improvements could make the biggest difference.

Top High-Risk Streets

One of the strongest findings in this project is that a relatively small number of streets account for a large share of Detroit's most severe crashes. Just 15 streets account for 34.6% of all fatal and serious-injury crashes, which shows that risk is highly concentrated rather than evenly distributed across the city.

Gratiot, Greenfield, and 7 Mile stand out as the most important corridors in the analysis. Other major arterials such as Grand River, Livernois, Outer, and McNichols also appear repeatedly among the city's highest-risk locations. These streets tend to be multi-lane roadways with moderate to high speed limits and limited traffic control, which may contribute to the frequency and severity of crashes.

Explanation of Clustering

The clustering of severe crashes makes sense when you look at how these roads function. Corridors like Gratiot, Greenfield, and 7 Mile are major arterials, which means they carry high volumes of traffic and often connect busy residential, commercial, and highway-linked areas. That combination creates more conflict points, more lane changes, and more opportunities for serious collisions.

Complex intersections also add to the danger. The report shows that intersections with formal traffic control, such as signals or stop signs, had a slightly higher share of serious or fatal crashes. This suggests that many of the worst locations are not just busy, but also difficult to navigate because of turning movements, merging, and multiple decision points.

The clustering also reflects the legacy of past planning decisions. Detroit's roadway system was shaped by infrastructure that prioritized vehicle throughput over safety, and that history still influences where crashes happen today. In that sense, the High Injury Network is not random — it reflects long-standing design patterns built into the city's streets.

Why These Areas Matter

These areas matter because they are where the city has the greatest opportunity to reduce serious harm. Since severe crashes are concentrated on a small number of corridors, improvements in these locations can have a larger impact than scattered changes across the whole city. That makes the High Injury Network a practical guide for targeted safety work.

The findings also matter for community health and daily mobility. People living near these corridors face higher exposure to dangerous conditions whether they are driving, walking, biking, or crossing the street. For that reason, these streets should be treated as priority locations for road diets, safer intersection design, and other safety-focused upgrades.

In short, the data shows that Detroit's most dangerous places are not just busy roads — they are places where roadway design, traffic volume, and intersection complexity combine to create serious risk.

Key Takeaway

Detroit's high-risk crashes are concentrated in a handful of major corridors, with Gratiot, Greenfield, and 7 Mile emerging as especially important. The clustering pattern shows that these locations deserve focused attention because they represent the strongest opportunities for preventing future serious injuries and fatalities.
